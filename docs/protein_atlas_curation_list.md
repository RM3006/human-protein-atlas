# Protein Atlas — Top 100 Curation List

## Purpose

This list defines the **short head** of the protein atlas: 100 human proteins that will receive hand-written, literary-quality story cards. The remaining ~20,000 reviewed human proteins are served by the same pipeline with LLM-generated descriptions over identical UI and data plumbing — every protein is in the atlas, only the editorial depth differs.

These 100 names exist because they are the proteins a casual visitor will plausibly search for — by name, by the disease they cause, or by the famous drug that targets them. They are also the proteins a recruiter clicking through the portfolio is most likely to recognize. Editorial quality here is more visible than editorial quality anywhere else, which is why this is the right place to concentrate human effort.

## How these proteins were chosen

A protein qualifies as "famous" for this list if it meets **at least one** of the following criteria. Most entries meet several.

1. **Newsworthy** — has appeared in mainstream science coverage (BRCA1, ACE2, PCSK9, TP53).
2. **Causes a well-known disease** — its dysfunction is named in textbooks or pediatric pamphlets (CFTR, dystrophin, PAH, huntingtin).
3. **A household drug target** — blockbuster or breakthrough therapies name it (EGFR for Tarceva, HMGCR for statins, TNF for Humira, ERBB2 for Herceptin).
4. **Builds something visible about you** — visible biology anyone can point to (keratin in hair and nails, collagen in skin, tyrosinase makes pigment).
5. **Makes everyday biology tick** — physiology people experience daily (insulin, lactase, hemoglobin, rhodopsin).
6. **Pedagogical anchor** — appears in any standard introductory biology lesson (myosin, actin, β-globin, the first-sequenced insulin).
7. **Cultural or historical note** — first-of-its-kind discoveries (insulin sequencing, prion discovery, PIEZO2 Nobel).

The list is also balanced thematically so a visitor exploring any region of the atlas — cancer, brain, blood, immunity, hormones — encounters hand-written cards rather than long stretches of auto-generated ones.

## What's deliberately not on the list

- **Housekeeping proteins** (GAPDH, ribosomal subunits, ubiquitin) — technical, not relatable.
- **Most of the ~800 GPCRs and ~500 kinases** — only iconic exemplars per family are included; the rest are still in the atlas, just auto-described.
- **Most rare-disease genes** — a small canonical set is included for breadth (NF1, Wilson's, hemochromatosis); the long tail of rare-disease genes gets the auto pipeline.
- **AlphaFold-famous structural proteins without a human story** — interesting to specialists, opaque to laypeople.

The other ~20,000 proteins still get full pages, full embeddings, full search, full neighbor lists. They are not second-class citizens of the *system* — they are second-class citizens of the *editorial pipeline*.

## Working notes

- All entries are **reviewed human proteins in UniProt** (Swiss-Prot, taxonomy 9606). Joining keys: official HGNC gene symbol and UniProt accession.
- Each tagline above doubles as the **subtitle line** under the protein name on its story card.
- A 3–5 sentence **narrative paragraph** will be hand-written for each as a separate authoring task — these are the tier-1 editorial slots. The other ~20,000 proteins get LLM-rewritten narratives over the same template.
- Themes are for editorial planning only. The warehouse schema does not need to encode them — an `is_curated` boolean column and a `tagline` column suffice.
- Total: 20 + 15 + 12 + 13 + 7 + 10 + 10 + 8 + 5 = **100**.
